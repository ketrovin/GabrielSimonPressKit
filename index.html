Gabriel Simon is a 44-year-old Mi’kmaw author who is redefining the modern zombie apocalypse genre. Based in Canada, Gabriel brings a powerful, authentic First Nations voice to speculative horror. He is the author of the rapidly expanding Deadlands series, an ongoing saga of zombie survival anthologies that includes Static and Sinew, The Dead and the Dustbowl, No Treaty With The Dead, and his latest release, The Dead and The Signal—with many more volumes planned for the future.

As a neurodivergent writer with ADHD and Aspergers, Gabriel views his condition not as a barrier, but as his creative engine. His neurodivergence fuels a hyper-focused writing process, enabling him to construct detailed, multi-layered anthologies that capture both the grand scale of societal collapse and the intimate human stories within it. Standing at a tall stature, Gabriel’s physical presence matches the bold, towering worlds he builds in his fiction.

His stories are celebrated for their gritty realism, historical and contemporary depth, and their focus on indigenous Canadian characters navigating the ultimate test of survival. When he isn't writing his next survival story, Gabriel is connecting with the horror community and developing new ideas for the Deadlands universe, which is far from finished.

Gabriel Simon is a 44-year-old Mi’kmaw author crafting some of the most unique, visceral, and culturally rich speculative horror today. Born and raised in Canada, Gabriel represents a rising wave of indigenous voices in genre fiction, weaving First Nations perspectives directly into the fabric of the zombie apocalypse. He is the mastermind behind the Deadlands series, an ongoing and far-from-finished horror universe that already spans four recently published books: the 1930s-set The Dead and the Dustbowl, the contemporary collections No Treaty With The Dead and Static and Sinew, and his latest release, The Dead and The Signal.

Gabriel's creative journey is deeply intertwined with his identity and his neurodivergent mind. Diagnosed with ADHD and Aspergers (Autism), he channels his natural tendency toward hyper-focus directly into his writing. Rather than seeing these traits as obstacles, Gabriel embraces them as the source of his intense world-building and rapid creative output. His unique cognitive style allows him to construct intricate, detail-oriented anthologies—often featuring 30 distinct stories per volume—that examine how different communities, particularly First Nations peoples, adapt and survive in a collapsed world.

At "quite tall" in stature, Gabriel's physical height is a memorable part of his identity, echoing the sweeping scale of his storytelling. His work stands tall in the horror community for its refusal to shy away from complex historical and social realities. In No Treaty With The Dead, for instance, Gabriel centers First Nations protagonists, exploring themes of resilience, historical trauma, and systemic survival in a world overrun by the undead. Meanwhile, The Dead and the Dustbowl merges historical fiction with creature horror, showing the struggles of the 1930s Dust Bowl through a dark, zombie-infested lens.

With his latest release, The Dead and The Signal, Gabriel continues to expand the Deadlands universe, delivering fresh terror and thoughtful representation in equal measure. With many more volumes planned, Gabriel aims to provide indigenous readers with heroes who look like them, while offering horror fans worldwide a raw, unvarnished look at what it truly takes to survive the end of the world.

FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE MI'KMAW AUTHOR GABRIEL SIMON LAUNCHES "THE DEAD AND THE SIGNAL," AN ANTHOLOGY EXPANDING HIS GROUNDBREAKING INDIGENOUS ZOMBIE APOCALYPSE UNIVERSE ATLANTIC CANADA – June 23, 2026 – Acclaimed Mi'kmaw author Gabriel Simon has officially released "The Dead and The Signal," the fourth volume in his popular "Deadlands" zombie apocalypse series. At 44 years old, Simon is breaking new ground in speculative fiction. Diagnosed with ADHD and Aspergers, Simon channels his neurodivergent traits into a hyper-focused creative process, resulting in highly detailed, rapidly written multi-perspective anthologies. Author Q&A

Gabriel Simon discusses his writing process, neurodivergence, and cultural roots.

My heritage is central to how I approach survival. In popular media, zombie apocalypses are usually viewed through a lens of total panic as modern comforts collapse. But for Indigenous people, we have already survived the collapse of our worlds, the loss of land, and systemic attempts to erase us. In No Treaty With The Dead, I write about characters who aren't just reacting to zombies; they are adapting, drawing strength from their communities, their history, and their deep relationship with the land. It’s about survival as an active, ongoing resistance.

I see my neurodivergence as my creative engine. Having ADHD and Aspergers allows me to enter a state of deep hyper-focus. When I am building the Deadlands universe, I don't just see one path; I see a web of thirty different scenarios happening concurrently. It allows me to write multiple short stories that connect in subtle, intricate ways. My mind naturally catalogs details, which helps me maintain continuity across hundreds of pages and decades of historical context, like in my 518-page book The Dead and the Dustbowl.

Zombies are the ultimate equalizer. When the dead walk, the artificial hierarchies of our world—wealth, bureaucratic power, social class—turn to dust instantly. What remains is raw human nature. I love exploring what happens in that vacuum. Who steps up? How do communities form? It allows me to show the best and worst of humanity under extreme pressure, and it makes space for stories of resilience that are often overlooked in traditional publishing.

The 1930s Dust Bowl was already an apocalyptic setting in real life. You had economic devastation, absolute poverty, and massive clouds of choking black dust rolling across the plains, destroying the land. People felt like it was the end of the world. Adding the undead to that environmental horror seemed like a natural, albeit terrifying, progression. It allowed me to write historical horror that addresses ecological collapse and human despair, showing that survival was a battle long before the dead rose.

The series is actually far from finished! I am just scratching the surface of this universe. I have outlines and concepts for many more books in the Deadlands saga. In the upcoming volumes, I plan to explore deeper, serialized character arcs and write longer narratives following some of the survivors introduced in the anthologies. There are still so many corners of this world to explore—especially how remote northern First Nations communities, coastal regions, and various historical eras adapt to the undead pandemic.
